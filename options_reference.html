How to use this reference

This document is structured as a self-contained reference, not a how-to guide for options trading. You will not trade options. You will use the consequences of other people's options positions to improve your reading of NQ futures price action during the London session.

Chapters 0–2 establish vocabulary and the core dealer-hedging mechanism. Chapters 3–7 build the option mechanics that explain why dealer behaviour takes the shape it does. Chapters 8–12 cover the market structure tools: GEX, VIX, walls, and the histogram. Chapters 13–16 are the London-specific application layer. Chapters 17–18 cover tools and conversion. The glossary and interactive section close the reference.

Scope note. The concepts in this reference operate on a daily and weekly timeframe. They provide context for your London session — not entry triggers. Triggers remain your existing volume profile, VWAP, and order flow playbooks.

What is an option?

An option is a contract giving the buyer the right — but not the obligation — to buy or sell something at a specific price before a specific date. The buyer pays a fee called the premium. The buyer's maximum possible loss is the premium paid.

Analogy — house insurance

You own a house worth €500,000. You buy insurance for €2,000 per year. The insurance gives you the right to sell your house for €500,000 even if it burns down and is worth only €50,000. You bought the right — not the obligation. Nothing happens → insurance expires worthless, you lost €2,000. Disaster strikes → your €2,000 policy is worth €450,000. A put option is exactly this: insurance on a stock or index.

Chapter 1

Why Futures Traders Care About Options

The options market and the futures market are not separate — they share the same underlying, and hedging in one drives flow in the other.

Every day, thousands of options contracts are traded on SPX and QQQ — the same indices NQ tracks. The institutions who sold those options, called dealers, must continuously trade NQ futures to protect themselves. Their hedging is mechanical, predictable, and directly affects price action on your chart whether you know it or not.

Central premise

You do not need to trade options. You need to understand how options positioning creates predictable dealer hedging flow in the NQ futures market — flow that shapes session volatility character, creates support and resistance zones, and amplifies or dampens moves beyond what order flow and auction theory alone would predict.

Why SPX and QQQ options affect NQ futures specifically

SPX, QQQ and NQ track the same US technology-heavy market and move together almost tick-for-tick during liquid hours. When large institutions hedge their SPX or QQQ options positions, they do so using NQ futures as the hedging vehicle — the most liquid, nearly 24-hour instrument available. On active days, this dealer hedging accounts for billions of dollars of NQ futures flow. That is structural force, not noise.

What knowing this gives you

A two-minute pre-session check tells you whether today is likely to be a grinding, mean-reverting day where fade setups outperform, or an explosive trending day where breakout and trend-follow setups outperform. One upstream context layer. No options trading required.

Chapter 2

The Casino: How Dealers Work

The casino analogy, decoded precisely.

The bet

You approach a casino and say: "I will pay you $50. If NQ rises 100 points today, you pay me." The casino takes your bet — but it does not gamble. It immediately buys NQ futures to cover its risk. As NQ moves during the session, the casino continuously adjusts that futures position. It does not care which direction NQ goes. It earns your $50 fee.

Step 1 — Your bet creates directional exposure

The casino now owes you money if NQ rises. In trading terms: it is effectively short NQ. To offset this, it immediately buys NQ futures. If NQ rises, its futures profit covers your payout. If NQ falls, it keeps your $50 and its futures loss is contained.

Step 2 — A one-time hedge is insufficient

Your bet's payout is not linear — it curves as NQ moves. Below +100 points the casino owes nothing; above +100 points the payout accelerates non-linearly. This means the casino's exposure (its delta) changes continuously as NQ moves. A static, one-time futures position does not remain accurate. It must adjust continuously.

Step 3 — Dynamic hedging

Because delta changes continuously, the casino must rebalance its futures position continuously. This is called dynamic hedging or delta hedging:

NQ directionEffect on casino's liabilityCasino's forced actionMarket effect
NQ risesLiability grows faster than linear hedge coversMust buy more NQ futuresAdds buying pressure
NQ fallsLiability shrinks; over-hedgedMust sell NQ futures backAdds selling pressure

Step 4 — How the casino profits

The casino profits from edge, not direction. The premium you paid is priced so the expected payout is less than the fee. It also earns bid-ask spread across many bettors and a volatility edge if markets move less than priced in.

Core conclusion

Dealers stay neutral by continuously rebalancing a futures hedge as NQ price moves. This rebalancing is mechanical — not based on any directional view. It is compelled by mathematics. The resulting futures flow is what you observe on your chart as support, resistance, acceleration, and pinning.

Whether this flow dampens or amplifies a move depends on whether the dealer is net long or short gamma — the subject of Chapter 9 (GEX).

Chapter 3

Why Does an Option's Price Change?

Three drivers: underlying price, time, and market nervousness.

An option's premium changes because of three independent forces. Understanding each helps explain how dealers are forced to rebalance their hedges — and when.

Driver 1 — Underlying price moves (Delta)

When NQ rises toward the strike of a call option, that call becomes more valuable. When NQ falls away, it loses value. The rate at which option price changes per $1 move in NQ is called delta. Delta is the primary driver of dealer hedging flow and is covered in depth in Chapter 4.

Driver 2 — Time passing (Theta / time decay)

Every day that passes, an option loses a portion of its value — even if NQ does not move at all. This erosion is called theta or time decay. It accelerates as expiry approaches: an option with 30 days remaining loses little per day; an option with 3 days remaining loses a great deal per day.

Analogy — lottery ticket

A lottery ticket bought on Monday for Friday's draw is worth something — four days of possibilities remain. By Thursday evening with hours left, the ticket holds almost nothing beyond its pure numerical value. Time is consuming its potential. An option is identical. A 5DTE option has five days of possibility priced into it. By the morning it expires (0DTE), only hours of possibility remain. This is why 0DTE options have almost no time value — only raw price sensitivity — and why their gamma is so extreme.

Driver 3 — Market nervousness (Implied Volatility)

Even if NQ price does not move and time does not pass, an option can change in value because of implied volatility (IV) — the market's expectation of future movement, embedded in option prices. When fear rises and participants buy put protection, option prices rise even if NQ is unchanged. This is IV expansion. VIX is a real-time measure of this.

When fear subsides and participants stop buying protection, option prices fall even if NQ is unchanged. This is IV contraction.

Analogy — insurance in a conflict zone

Your home insurance is cheap in a quiet neighbourhood. If fighting breaks out in an adjacent area, insurance companies raise prices everywhere immediately — not because anything has happened to your house, but because the risk environment has changed. Implied volatility is exactly this. When VIX spikes, option prices rise across all strikes because the market environment has become more dangerous, regardless of whether NQ has moved yet.

Vol crush

Vol crush occurs when implied volatility drops sharply after a known event resolves — an earnings release, a Federal Reserve decision, a macro shock that clears. Options that were expensive going into the event collapse in price, often even if NQ moved in the "right" direction.

For NQ futures traders: vol crush causes vanna flows (Chapter 7) to reverse rapidly as dealers must rebalance. A sharp VIX drop — even in a downtrend — can trigger explosive bear market rallies. The violence of such moves is explained by the mechanical reversal of forced dealer selling becoming forced dealer buying.

Chapter 4

What Is Delta? Three Ways to Read It

The same number — read as price sensitivity, probability, and hedge ratio.

Delta is the single most important number linking the options market to NQ futures. It ranges from 0.00 to 1.00 for calls and 0.00 to −1.00 for puts. Depending on context, it can be read three distinct ways.

Reading 1 — Price sensitivity

Delta tells you how much the option's price changes when NQ moves one point. A call with delta 0.50 gains $0.50 in value for every $1 NQ rises. A call with delta 0.20 gains only $0.20. Higher delta = option moves more in lockstep with NQ.

Reading 2 — Probability proxy

Delta approximates the probability that the option expires in the money. A 0.50 delta call has roughly a 50% chance of being in the money at expiry. A 0.10 delta call has roughly a 10% chance. Deep OTM (low delta) options are cheap because they represent long shots.

Reading 3 — Hedge ratio (most important for you)

Delta tells a dealer how many NQ futures contracts to hold per option contract sold. If a dealer sold 1,000 contracts of a 0.50 delta call, they must hold the equivalent of 500 NQ futures contracts. This is the direct mechanical link between options positioning and NQ futures flow.

Why ATM delta (~0.50) is most dangerous for dealers

When an option is at-the-money — when NQ price is exactly at the strike — gamma is at its highest. Gamma is the rate of change of delta. High gamma means delta is changing rapidly with every NQ tick, which means dealers must rebalance their hedge most aggressively for every small price move.

Deep ITM options (delta near 1.00) change little in delta — the dealer already holds near-full exposure and small moves require minimal adjustment. Deep OTM options (delta near 0.00) also require little adjustment. It is ATM options — at the strike — where every tick forces substantial dealer trading. This is the mechanical origin of Call Walls and Put Walls.

Key principle

The largest dealer hedging flows occur at and near major strikes because that is where ATM delta, and therefore gamma, is highest. This creates observable support and resistance zones on your NQ chart — not from order book limits, but from mathematical compulsion.

Chapter 5

The Dealer Hedging Story

Why a static hedge fails, and why continuous rebalancing creates the flows you observe.

The dealer's problem: curved obligation, linear hedge

When a dealer sells an option, they acquire a curved obligation — the option's payoff grows non-linearly as NQ moves past the strike. But a futures contract is a linear instrument: it gains or loses at a constant rate regardless of how far price moves. This mismatch between a curved obligation and a linear hedge is the origin of all dealer flow.

As NQ rises to 21200, the curved // obligation exceeds the linear hedge → dealer must BUY more futures. // As NQ falls to 20900, the linear hedge exceeds the obligation // → dealer is over-hedged → must SELL futures back.

Why dynamic hedging is the only solution

Because the option's payoff curve continuously changes shape as NQ moves, a one-time hedge becomes inaccurate the instant price moves. The dealer must rebalance the futures position continuously to keep it tracking the curve. The frequency and size of each rebalancing trade is determined by gamma — the higher the gamma, the faster the curve changes, the more frequently the dealer must trade.

The mechanical feedback

The forced nature of this flow

Dealers do not choose to buy or sell NQ based on a directional view. They are compelled to trade by mathematics — the mismatch between the shape of their option obligation and the shape of their hedge. This forced, mechanical trading is what appears on your chart as walls, pinning, and momentum acceleration.

Chapter 6

Price Acceleration: The Turbocharger

Dealers do not initiate directional moves — they amplify or dampen them.

Dealers as participants in momentum, not as initiators

A directional move in NQ begins from some other cause: an order flow imbalance, a news event, a large institutional participant taking a position. Once the move begins, dealer hedging can dramatically amplify or reduce it. They are the turbocharger or the shock absorber — not the engine.

The feedback loop in negative GEX (amplifying)

When GEX is negative — dealers are short gamma — the following loop operates:

  1. NQ begins to rally for any reason
  2. Dealers' delta rises → they must buy NQ futures to rebalance
  3. That buying adds upward pressure to NQ
  4. NQ rises further → dealers' delta rises again → they must buy more
  5. The loop continues until price reaches the next significant GEX level (a wall)

The same loop operates in reverse on declines: NQ falls → dealers sell → more decline → dealers sell more → accelerates downward.

Analogy — the casino always rides momentum

In a normal casino, the house wins by having a mathematical edge over the player. In the options market, dealers win differently: by always being on both sides of momentum. When GEX is negative, the dealer buys into rallies and sells into declines — always with the prevailing direction, never against it. They do not need to predict which way NQ goes. Once a direction is established, dealer flow amplifies it mechanically until the next wall level stops it.

The damping loop in positive GEX

When GEX is positive — dealers are long gamma — the feedback loop reverses:

  • NQ rises → dealers' delta rises → they must sell NQ futures → selling dampens the rally → NQ oscillates back
  • NQ falls → dealers' delta falls → they must buy NQ futures → buying catches the decline → NQ bounces back
The two regime states

Positive GEX: dealers are shock absorbers. Every move away from equilibrium triggers a dealer trade in the opposite direction. Price oscillates. Fades work.

Negative GEX: dealers are turbochargers. Every move in any direction triggers a dealer trade in the same direction. Trends extend. Breakouts follow through. Do not fade strong moves.

Chapter 7

Delta, Gamma & Vanna

The three Greek names relevant to NQ futures traders — summarised.

GreekWhat it measuresWhen it forces dealer actionWhy it matters for NQ

Gamma (Γ)Rate of change of delta per NQ point. Highest when ATM.Highest at strike price (ATM)Creates walls, pinning, price magnetism at major strikes
Vanna (V)Rate of change of delta per unit change in implied volatility (VIX)When VIX spikes or collapsesForces NQ selling when VIX rises (amplifying selloffs); reverses on VIX collapse (violent bear rallies)
Analogy — gamma

Imagine carrying a ball that becomes heavier the closer you get to a wall. Near the wall — near a strike price — every small step forces you to drop more weight to stay balanced. Far from the wall, large steps barely change the load. Gamma is the same: the closer NQ is to a major strike, the more dealer re-hedging occurs per point of price movement.

Analogy — vanna

Imagine your hedging requirements change not because you moved, but because the room temperature changed. Standing completely still, you must adjust your balance because the environment shifted. Vanna operates this way: a VIX spike forces dealer rebalancing in NQ even when NQ price has not moved significantly yet. This is why a fear spike can trigger NQ selling before price has broken any structural level — the environment changed, and dealers had no choice but to act.

The vanna feedback on selloffs

During a selloff: NQ falls → fear rises → VIX spikes → vanna forces dealers to sell additional NQ → price falls further → VIX rises more → more forced selling. This self-reinforcing loop is what drives crash dynamics and explains why selloffs accelerate at a rate beyond what gamma alone would produce.

The loop terminates when VIX reaches a level where it can only go lower. At that point, vanna reverses sign and forces aggressive dealer buying — the source of violent bear market rallies that appear to come from nowhere.

Analogy — grenade vs brick wall

A 5DTE option is a grenade with the pin still in: enormous potential energy, stable in your hand, usable for planning. The walls it creates are solid and predictable — brick walls that hold all week. A 0DTE option is a grenade with the pin already pulled: fully live energy, detonates at any movement. The walls it creates exist only for one trading session — sandcastles that disappear at the daily close.

Why 0DTE volume exploded

Before 2022, SPX options had three expirations per week. The CBOE added daily Monday-through-Friday expirations in 2022. Retail and institutional traders discovered that 0DTE options were inexpensive to buy and could multiply in value within hours. Volume exploded; by 2023, 0DTE options exceeded 50% of all SPX options volume on active days. This created the extreme intraday gamma dynamics now felt in NQ each US afternoon session.

Chapter 9

GEX: The Session Dial

Gamma Exposure in one number — what type of day is today?

GEX (Gamma Exposure) is the net result of every dealer's gamma hedging combined into a single number. Positive GEX means dealers are net long gamma — they act as shock absorbers. Negative GEX means dealers are net short gamma — they act as turbochargers. Near zero means no systematic dealer interference.

Negative GEX
  • Turbocharger mode
  • Trends extend
  • Breakouts follow through
  • Do not fade strong moves
  • Trend playbooks
Near Zero
  • No dealer interference
  • Pure price discovery
  • Auction theory dominant
  • Read the open carefully
  • Both setups possible
Positive GEX
  • Shock absorber mode
  • Fades work well
  • Mean-reversion dominates
  • False breaks are common
  • Fade playbooks

GEX predicts volatility character, not direction. Positive GEX does not mean NQ rises — it means NQ oscillates. Negative GEX does not mean NQ falls — it means whatever direction NQ picks, it travels further than structure alone would suggest.

Research finding

SqueezeMetrics research (2017) found that when GEX was at its highest positive levels, average daily SPX returns had a 1-day standard deviation of approximately 0.55%. When GEX turned negative, the same measure rose to 3–6% in extreme cases. The difference between a compression day and an expansion day is visible in the GEX reading before the session begins.

Chapter 10

VIX: How It Is Derived

The fear index — what it measures, how it is calculated, and how to use it.

Derivation

The CBOE reads real-time bid-ask prices of hundreds of SPX call and put options with expirations between 23 and 37 days out. For each option, it calculates the implied volatility — the expected future volatility that would make the option's current price fair. It then weights all of these implied volatilities together and expresses the result as an annualised percentage.

Working rule: VIX ÷ 16 ≈ expected daily percentage move in SPX. VIX at 16 implies roughly ±1% per day. VIX at 32 implies roughly ±2% per day.

What VIX is not

VIX is not directional. It does not say whether NQ will rise or fall. It measures the expected magnitude of movement. It is also not a prediction — it measures what current option prices imply today. Option buyers and sellers may be systematically wrong about the future. But it is the best real-time measure of market fear and expected volatility available.

Walls beyond the primary range

The space between Put Wall and Call Wall is the expected range for the session. When that range holds, price oscillates between the two levels. When it breaks, the next wall in the histogram becomes your target.

Trading application

If the Put Wall (20,800) breaks: the air pocket between 20,900 and 20,800 has no dealer cushion — price falls quickly. The secondary Put Wall (20,600) is your target and your next support reference. Chapter 14

Pre-Session Checklist: 5DTE Walls

Three questions before London open — then one line in Obsidian.

The three questions

  1. Where is NQ price relative to the 5DTE walls? Is price inside the range (between Put Wall and Call Wall)? Is it pressing toward a wall? Did it break above or below overnight?
  2. What is VIX doing? Level (below 15 / 15–20 / 20–30 / 30+) and direction (rising or falling vs yesterday's close)?
  3. What is the overall GEX reading? Positive histogram = shock absorber day. Negative net GEX = amplifier day. Near zero = read the open carefully.

Chapter 17

QQQ → NQ Conversion

The relationship between QQQ option levels and NQ futures price.

The formula

QQQ is designed to trade at approximately 1/40th of the NDX (NASDAQ 100 cash index). NDX and NQ futures trade very close to each other — typically within 10–40 points depending on the futures roll premium. For GEX level planning, treat NDX ≈ NQ.

Conversion rules

NQ level ≈ QQQ level × 40

QQQ level ≈ NQ level ÷ 40

Example: QQQ Call Wall at 480 → NQ ≈ 480 × 40 = 19,200
Example: QQQ Put Wall at 460 → NQ ≈ 460 × 40 = 18,400
Example: NQ at 21,100 → QQQ ≈ 21,100 ÷ 40 = 527.50

The TLADe indicator handles this conversion automatically. You do not need to calculate it manually during the session.

Do you need to monitor QQQ directly?

Not if you are using the TLADe indicator on your NQ chart. The indicator fetches QQQ option data and converts all walls, Zero Gamma, Max Pain, and Expected Move bands to NQ price terms. Use Barchart's QQQ GEX page as a secondary sanity check or for a more detailed histogram view before the session.

Nuance

The ×40 formula is an approximation. The precise NQ-to-NDX relationship shifts as futures roll quarterly. TLADe accounts for this in its conversion logic (using a 40-point NQ-NDX spread). For most London trading purposes the approximation is accurate enough. The TLADe ticker switcher also allows direct NQ, NDX, or QQQ selection — eliminating the need for manual conversion.

Put optionRight to sell at strike. Dealers short puts must buy NQ as price falls toward strike = Put Wall.
Strike priceSpecific NQ price level an option targets. High open interest at a strike = gamma wall on chart.
PremiumFee paid to buy an option. Max loss for buyer. Higher VIX = higher premiums.

Delta (Δ)Rate of option price change per $1 NQ move. Also: probability proxy and dealer hedge ratio. ATM ≈ 0.50 = highest gamma = highest dealer risk.

Theta (θ)Time decay. Daily erosion of option value as expiry approaches. Accelerates near expiry. 0DTE has almost no theta — only delta remains.
VannaRate of delta change when VIX changes. Active when VIX spikes. Forces dealer NQ selling into selloffs. Reverses violently on VIX collapse = bear market rallies.

Dynamic hedgingContinuous adjustment of dealer NQ futures position as price moves. The engine behind all GEX effects.
