Most Important
What Actually IS an Option?
🏠 ANALOGY — HOUSE INSURANCE

You own a house worth €500,000. You buy insurance for €2,000/year. The insurance gives you the right to sell your house for €500,000 — even if it burns down and is worth only €50,000 afterwards.

You bought the right — not the obligation. Nothing happens → insurance expires worthless, you lost €2,000. Disaster strikes → your €2,000 policy is worth €450,000. A put option is exactly this — insurance on a stock or index.

📗
CALL Option

Right to BUY at strike price. Bought when expecting market to go UP. NQ rises above strike → valuable. Stays below → expires worthless.

📕
PUT Option

Right to SELL at strike price. Insurance against market going DOWN. NQ falls below strike → valuable. Stays above → expires worthless.

💡
Key Terms

Strike Price: The specific price the option targets (e.g. 21000 NQ).
Expiry: The date the option runs out. After this, worthless if unused.
Premium: Fee paid to buy the option. Your maximum possible loss.
ITM (In The Money): Has real intrinsic value right now.
OTM (Out of The Money): Only has potential value — intrinsically worthless today.

"You are NOT learning to trade options.
You're learning how other people's options affect YOUR NQ charts."
01
WHY FUTURES TRADERS CARE
01
Chapter One
You're Already Swimming in Options Water
🎯
The Big Idea

Every day, thousands of options on SPX and QQQ are traded — the same indices NQ tracks. The institutions who sold those options (dealers) must continuously trade NQ futures to protect themselves. Their mechanical, predictable hedging trades affect your price action constantly, whether you know it or not.

"You don't need to trade options to be affected by them."
🤔
Why do SPX/QQQ options affect NQ futures specifically?

SPX, QQQ and NQ all track the same US tech-heavy market and move together almost tick-for-tick. When large institutions hedge their SPX/QQQ options, they use NQ futures as the hedging vehicle. On busy days, this dealer hedging accounts for billions of dollars of NQ flow. That's a structural force — not noise.

✅
What knowing this gives you

A quick pre-session check tells you whether today is likely to be a grinding, mean-reverting day (fade setups work) or an explosive trending day (breakout setups work). One extra layer of context. No options trading required.

THE CASINO — HOW DEALERS WORK
02
Chapter Two
How Dealers Stay Neutral — Step by Step
🎰 THE BET

You say to a casino: "I'll pay $50. If NQ goes up 100 points today, you pay me." The casino takes your bet but doesn't gamble — they immediately buy NQ futures to cover their risk. As NQ moves during the day, they continuously adjust that futures position. They don't care which direction NQ goes. They earn your $50 fee.

1️⃣
Step 1 — Your bet creates their risk

They now owe you money if NQ rises. They are effectively short NQ. To offset this: they immediately buy NQ futures. NQ goes up → futures profit covers your payout. NQ falls → they keep your $50.

2️⃣
Step 2 — A one-time hedge fails

Your bet's payout isn't linear — it's curved. Below +100 pts they owe nothing; above +100 pts payout accelerates. So their exposure (delta) changes as NQ moves. One static hedge doesn't stay accurate for long. They must adjust continuously.

3️⃣
Step 3 — Dynamic hedging

📈 NQ up → their liability grows → BUY more NQ futures
📉 NQ down → their liability shrinks → SELL NQ futures back

This continuous adjustment is called dynamic hedging. Every adjustment is a real NQ futures trade. The casino is not predicting direction — it is manufacturing your payoff in the futures market.

💰
Step 4 — How they profit

Edge, not direction: the premium you paid is priced so their expected payout is less than the fee. They also earn bid-ask spread and volatility edge. Dealers stay neutral by continuously rebalancing a futures hedge as price moves. Their rebalancing IS your price action.

03
WHY DOES AN OPTION'S PRICE CHANGE?
03
Chapter Three
Three Things That Change an Option's Value
🎓
Three Drivers of Option Price

An option's price (premium) changes because of three things: the underlying price moving, time passing, and market nervousness changing. In options language these are called Delta, Theta, and Implied Volatility. You don't need to calculate them — just understand what they mean.

1️⃣
Driver 1 — PRICE MOVES (Delta)

If you own a call on NQ at 21000 and NQ rises to 21100, your call is now worth more because it's closer to (or already past) your target price. If NQ falls, your call loses value. This sensitivity of the option's price to the underlying price moving is called delta. Full chapter on delta next.

2️⃣
Driver 2 — TIME PASSING (Theta / Time Decay)

Every single day that passes, your option is worth a little less — even if NQ doesn't move at all. Why? Because an option is partly a bet on what might happen. The more time remaining, the more things could happen. As time runs out, that possibility shrinks.

This daily erosion is called theta (time decay). Theta accelerates as expiry approaches — the last few days of an option's life, it loses value fastest.

🎟️ ANALOGY — THE LOTTERY TICKET

Imagine you buy a lottery ticket for Friday's draw. On Monday, that ticket is worth something — you have 4 days of possibilities. By Thursday evening, with only hours left, the ticket is worth almost nothing extra beyond the pure number it holds. Time is melting away its potential.

An option is the same. A 5DTE (5-day) option has 5 days of possibility priced into it. By the morning it expires (0DTE), only hours of possibility remain. This is why 0DTE options have almost no time value — only raw price sensitivity remains. And that's why their gamma is so extreme (more in Chapter 8).

3️⃣
Driver 3 — MARKET NERVOUSNESS (Implied Volatility)

Even if NQ price doesn't move and time doesn't pass, an option can change in value because of implied volatility (IV) — the market's expectation of future movement embedded in option prices.

When market participants get nervous and buy more put protection, option prices rise — even if the underlying hasn't moved. This is called an IV expansion. VIX is a measure of this.

When fear subsides and people stop buying protection, option prices fall even if the underlying stays put. This is called IV contraction.

🏥 ANALOGY — INSURANCE IN A WAR ZONE

Imagine you live in a quiet neighbourhood. Your home insurance is cheap because nothing bad ever happens there. Now imagine a war breaks out in the next town. Suddenly, everyone wants insurance. The insurance company raises prices dramatically — not because anything has happened to your house yet, but because the environment is now far more dangerous.

That's implied volatility. When markets get nervous (VIX rises), option prices go up everywhere — even for options far from the current price — because the risk environment changed. The "insurance" just got more expensive.

When the war ends and calm returns, insurance prices drop back. That sudden drop in option prices after a fear event resolves is called "vol crush."

💥
Vol Crush — What It Is and Why It Matters

Vol crush happens when implied volatility drops sharply after a known event (earnings, Fed decision, a macro shock resolving). Options that were expensive going into the event suddenly collapse in price — even if the underlying moved in the "right" direction.

Example: NQ rises 200 points after a Fed meeting. Your call should profit. But IV drops from 28 to 16 after the uncertainty is resolved. The premium in your option collapses. You might break even or even lose.

For you as a futures trader: vol crush means a sharp VIX drop can cause vanna flows to reverse rapidly (dealers must now buy back what they sold), creating explosive bear-market rallies even in downtrends.

📌
SUMMARY: Three things move option prices: (1) NQ price moving = Delta effect. (2) Time passing = Theta / time decay — accelerates near expiry. (3) Market nervousness changing = IV expansion or vol crush. All three affect how dealers must hedge.
04
WHAT IS DELTA? — THREE WAYS TO READ IT
04
Chapter Four
Delta — The Single Most Important Number
🔑
Delta has three meanings — all useful

The same number (delta) can be read three different ways, and each gives you different useful information. The number ranges from 0.00 to 1.00 for calls, and 0.00 to -1.00 for puts.

1️⃣
Reading 1 — Price Sensitivity

Delta tells you how much the option price changes when NQ moves 1 point. A call with delta 0.50 gains $0.50 in value for every $1 NQ rises. A call with delta 0.20 only gains $0.20 per NQ point. Higher delta = option moves more in lockstep with NQ.

2️⃣
Reading 2 — Probability Proxy

Delta is a rough estimate of the probability that the option expires in the money. A delta 0.50 call has roughly a 50% chance of being in the money at expiry. A delta 0.10 call has roughly a 10% chance. This is why deep OTM (low delta) options are cheap — long shots. ATM (delta ~0.50) options are the fair toss.

3️⃣
Reading 3 — Hedge Ratio (Most Important for You)

Delta tells a dealer how many NQ futures contracts they must hold to stay neutral for each option contract they sold. If they sold a 0.50 delta call, they must hold 0.50 contracts of NQ futures worth. If they sold 1000 contracts of 0.50 delta calls, they hold 500 NQ futures contracts. This is the mechanical link between the options market and your futures market.

🔥
Why ATM Delta (~0.50) Is Most Dangerous for Dealers

When an option is at-the-money (NQ price exactly at the strike), gamma is at its highest. Gamma is the rate of change of delta. High gamma means delta is changing rapidly with every NQ tick — which means dealers must rebalance their hedge more frequently and more aggressively.

Deep ITM options (delta close to 1.00) don't change much in delta — the dealer already holds almost full exposure, and small price moves don't force much adjustment. Deep OTM options (delta near 0) also don't need much adjusting.

But ATM options? Every small move forces the dealer to buy or sell significantly. This is why the largest dealer hedging flows happen right at major strikes — exactly where your Call Walls and Put Walls form.

THE DEALER HEDGING STORY
05
Chapter Five
Why Static Hedging Fails — and Why Dynamic Hedging Creates Price Flow
🧩
The Dealer's Problem

When a dealer sells an option, they take on a curved obligation. The option's payout is not a straight line — it curves as NQ moves. But a futures contract IS a straight line. This mismatch between a curved liability and a linear hedge is the source of all dealer flow.

📉 CURVED OBLIGATION vs STRAIGHT HEDGE
Dealer's curved obligation (option payoff)    Dealer's linear futures hedge
208002090021000211002120021300
At NQ 21000 the lines cross. Above 21000: curved obligation exceeds straight hedge → dealer must BUY MORE FUTURES. Below 21000: straight hedge exceeds obligation → dealer must SELL FUTURES BACK.
⚙️
Why Dynamic Hedging Is the Only Solution

Since the option's payoff curve keeps changing shape as NQ moves, a one-time hedge becomes inaccurate the moment price moves. The dealer has two choices: accept the risk (they won't) or continuously rebalance the futures position to keep tracking the curve.

They choose to rebalance continuously. This is dynamic hedging. The frequency and size of each rebalancing trade is determined by gamma — the higher the gamma, the faster the curve changes, the more frequently they must trade.

🔄
The Mechanical Feedback

Here is the direct, unavoidable consequence for your chart:

📈 NQ rises → option curve rises faster than the linear hedge → dealer must BUY futures to catch up → adds fuel to the rally

📉 NQ falls → option curve drops faster than the linear hedge → dealer must SELL futures → adds fuel to the decline

Whether this buying/selling amplifies or dampens the move depends on whether the dealer is long or short gamma (GEX positive or negative). The direction of the mechanical trade is the same — only the net effect changes.

🔑
KEY: Dealers don't choose to buy or sell based on their view of the market. They are forced to trade by mathematics — the shape of their option obligation versus the shape of their hedge. This forced, mechanical trading is what you see on your chart as support, resistance, walls, and acceleration.
06
PRICE ACCELERATION — THE TURBOCHARGER
06
Chapter Six
How Dealers Add Fuel to an Already-Moving Fire
🚀
Dealers don't cause moves — they turbocharge them

An important distinction: dealers don't start directional moves in NQ. A move begins from some other catalyst — a news event, order flow imbalance, or just a large buyer/seller appearing. But once the move starts, dealer hedging can dramatically amplify it. They are the turbocharger, not the ignition.

🔄
The Feedback Loop — When GEX Is Negative

When GEX is negative (dealers are short gamma):

1. NQ begins to rally for any reason
2. Dealers' delta rises → they must buy NQ futures to rebalance
3. That buying adds more upward pressure
4. NQ rises further
5. Dealers' delta rises again → they must buy even more futures
6. Loop continues until price reaches the next significant GEX level (wall)

The same loop works in reverse on declines: NQ falls → dealers sell → more decline → dealers sell more → accelerates downward.

🎰 ANALOGY — THE CASINO WINS BOTH WAYS

In a normal casino, the house wins by having a mathematical edge — slightly better odds than the players. But in the options market, the dealer wins differently: by always being on both sides of momentum.

When GEX is negative: the market moves up → dealers buy (going long with it). The market moves down → dealers sell (going short with it). The dealer is always momentum's passenger — amplifying it in whatever direction it goes. They don't pick the direction. They just always get to ride for free, with their premium already collected.

The casino doesn't need to predict which way NQ goes. They just need the market to move. The more it moves, the more they hedge, the more it moves. This is why negative GEX days feel like price is on rails — once a direction is established, dealer flow amplifies it mechanically until the next wall stops it.

🛡️
The Reverse — When GEX Is Positive (Dampening)

When GEX is positive, the feedback loop runs backwards:

NQ rises → dealers' delta rises → they must SELL futures to rebalance → selling pressure dampens the rally → NQ oscillates back.

NQ falls → dealers' delta falls → they must BUY futures to rebalance → buying support catches the fall → NQ bounces back.

Positive GEX = dealers are the shock absorbers. Negative GEX = dealers are the turbochargers.

07
DELTA, GAMMA & VANNA — QUICK SUMMARY
07
Chapter Seven
The Three Greek Names You Need
DELTA
How much the dealer's NQ hedge must change when NQ moves $1. Changes continuously as price moves. The engine of all dealer hedging flow. Covered deeply in Chapter 4.
🔵 Range: 0 (deep OTM) → 1.0 (deep ITM). ATM ≈ 0.50 = highest gamma risk for dealers.
GAMMA
How fast delta changes per NQ point moved. Highest when price is at the strike (ATM). High gamma = dealer must rebalance aggressively for every small price tick.
🟠 WHY YOU CARE: High gamma at a strike = heavy dealer flow = walls, pinning, price magnetism.
VANNA
How much delta changes when VIX (fear) changes — NOT when price moves. When VIX spikes, dealers rebalance NQ even without a price move triggering it.
🟣 WHY YOU CARE: NQ falling + VIX rising = vanna forces extra NQ selling. Selloffs accelerate when fear spikes.
🏋️ ANALOGY — GAMMA

Imagine you're carrying a ball that gets heavier the closer you get to a wall. Near the wall (= near a strike price), every small step forces you to drop more weight to stay balanced. Far from the wall, big steps barely change anything. That's gamma: the closer NQ is to a major strike, the more dealer re-hedging happens per point of movement.

🌡️ ANALOGY — VANNA

Imagine your hedging needs change not because you moved, but because the room temperature changed. Even standing perfectly still, you need to adjust your balance because the environment shifted. That's vanna: VIX rising forces dealer re-hedging even when NQ price itself hasn't moved much yet. This is why a VIX spike can trigger NQ selling before price has broken any level.

0DTE vs 5DTE — THE GRENADE VS THE BRICK WALL
08
Chapter Eight
Two Types of Options — Completely Different Behaviour
💣
0DTE — Zero Days

Options expiring today. Almost no time value left. Pure price sensitivity. Gamma is at its absolute maximum — tiny NQ moves force massive dealer re-hedging. Created daily (Mon–Fri on SPX). Exploded in volume since 2021.

🧱
5DTE — Five Days

Options expiring in one week (weekly options). Still have meaningful time value. Gamma is significant but not extreme. Built on Monday, expire the following Friday. These are what the TLADe indicator uses by default. The structural foundation of your pre-session planning.

💣 ANALOGY — THE GRENADE

A 5DTE option is like a grenade with the pin still in. It has enormous potential energy, but it's stable. The walls it creates are solid and predictable — like brick walls that hold all week.

A 0DTE option is like a grenade with the pin already pulled. Its energy is fully live and can detonate at any moment. The slightest movement triggers explosive dealer hedging. By definition, whatever wall it creates only exists for that one trading day — then it's gone.

0DTE walls are sandcastles. 5DTE walls are brick walls.

📈
Why 0DTE Exploded in Popularity

Before 2021, SPX only had 3 expirations per week. In 2022 CBOE added daily SPX expirations (Mon–Fri). Retail traders discovered that 0DTE options were extremely cheap to buy and could multiply in value within hours. Volume exploded. By 2023, 0DTE options accounted for more than half of all SPX options volume daily. This created the extreme intraday gamma dynamics you now feel in NQ every afternoon during NY RTH.

⚡
FOR YOUR TRADING: 0DTE walls form and vanish within a single day. 5DTE walls are your weekly structural map. For London session planning (08:00–12:00 CET), 5DTE is all you need. 0DTE only matters if you trade NY RTH (15:30 CET onwards). More detail in Chapter 13.

VIX — HOW IT'S DERIVED
10
Chapter Ten
VIX — The Market's Live Fear Thermometer
⚙️
How VIX is derived

The CBOE reads real-time prices of hundreds of SPX call and put options with expirations 23–37 days out. For each option it asks: "How much expected volatility does this price imply?" It weights all of these together and expresses the result as an annualised percentage. VIX ÷ 16 ≈ expected daily % move. VIX 16 → ~1%/day. VIX 32 → ~2%/day.

📍
What VIX is NOT

VIX is not directional. It doesn't say NQ goes up or down. It measures expected violence of movement. Also: a rising VIX activates vanna flows (Chapter 7). A falling VIX reverses them — which is why bear market rallies after VIX peaks are explosive.

⚡

PUT WALL, CALL WALL, ZERO GAMMA, MAX PAIN
11
Chapter Eleven
The Four Levels That Define the Day's Map
📗
CALL WALL

Strike with most call open interest. Resistance ceiling. Dealers short calls must sell NQ futures as price rises toward this strike. Mechanical selling = wall.

Break above Call Wall: dealers flip to buying. Move accelerates upward. New Call Wall resets higher.

📕
PUT WALL

Strike with most put open interest. Support floor. Dealers short puts must buy NQ futures as price falls toward this strike. Mechanical buying = wall.

Break below Put Wall: gamma collapses as puts go deep ITM. Mechanical buying fades. Fast drop to next put wall.

🔄
ZERO GAMMA

The price where aggregate dealer gamma crosses from positive to negative. The regime switch.

Above Zero Gamma → stabilising (shock absorber). Below Zero Gamma → amplifying (turbocharger). Crossing this level is the most important intraday regime signal.

😖
MAX PAIN

Strike where total value of all outstanding options is at its lowest — where options sellers profit most. Price gravitates here as expiry approaches. Strongest effect in final hour of Friday expiry. Less relevant for London open, critical for Friday afternoon awareness.

📐
Expected Move Bands

The TLADe indicator also shows statistical expected move bands for the week — roughly ±1 standard deviation of expected NQ range. Moves outside these bands are unusual. Think of them as the outer boundaries of "normal" weekly behaviour. Rare moves beyond them often involve vanna-driven cascade dynamics.

How to read the histogram

Tall bar: Strong wall. Dealer hedging aggressive here. Price stalls, struggles, often reverses. High-conviction fade zone on approach.

Short bar: Weak level. Dealer hedging thin. Price passes through easily. Don't expect support or resistance here.

Gap / no bar: Air pocket. Zero dealer cushion. If price enters this zone, it moves quickly in either direction with nothing to slow it. These are the zones where the fastest NQ moves happen. Also your best entry zones for momentum trades after a wall breaks.

🎯
Walls Beyond the Primary Range — Why They Matter

Most days, price stays between Put Wall and Call Wall. But when a wall breaks, the next wall becomes your map:

If the Put Wall breaks: The secondary Put Wall below is your next support and your trade target. The air pocket between Put Wall and Secondary Put Wall tells you how far this move can run before encountering new mechanical buying. This is how you set targets on breakdown days.

If the Call Wall breaks: The secondary Call Wall above becomes resistance and your target. A break above the primary Call Wall followed by continuation to the secondary is a powerful multi-R move — dealers are forced buyers all the way through the air pocket above.

FOR LONDON TRADERS — WHY 0DTE DOESN'T MATTER
13
Chapter Thirteen
London Session Context — Focus on 5DTE, Not 0DTE
🕐
The Time Zone Reality

London Open Session: 08:00–10:00 CET = 02:00–04:00 ET
London Mid Session: 10:00–12:00 CET = 04:00–06:00 ET

0DTE options are built for US RTH (Regular Trading Hours). The options open, the market makers set their books, the heavy volume begins. But during your London session, the US market hasn't even opened yet — 0DTE activity is minimal.

📅
When 0DTE Actually Becomes Active

0DTE options begin printing serious volume approximately 30–60 minutes after US cash market open (09:30 ET = 15:30 CET). Peak 0DTE gamma impact is typically between 14:00–21:00 CET — which is your late session or after your trading window. During your London open, 0DTE walls either don't exist yet (new ones haven't formed) or are leftovers from the previous day's close (which carry minimal gamma).

🧱
Why 5DTE Walls Are More Useful for London Open

5DTE walls are set when weekly options are opened (typically Sunday evening or Monday open). They are stable all week. By London open on Monday, they are fully established. By Wednesday, they are at their most predictable (maximum time spent at each strike).

The key insight: London session price action happens within the structural framework of 5DTE walls. These walls define the week's expected range. Your London open is playing out inside the map that was drawn on Sunday night.

Behaviour at 5DTE Wall Positions

Scenario A — Price Between Walls (Inside the Range)
Normal rotation day expected. Dealers acting as shock absorbers from both sides. Your VWAP mean-reversion and stop-run setups get a tailwind. Target the walls as fade levels. Trust the structure — it holds most of the time.

Scenario B — Price Pressing Call Wall (From Below)
Mechanical dealer selling is building. High probability fade zone. Don't chase longs into the wall. Look for your existing trigger (sweep+reclaim, absorption at the wall) to take a short back toward VWAP or Put Wall. If VIX is low and GEX is positive, wall has high chance of holding.

Scenario C — Price Broken Above Call Wall Overnight
Dealers have flipped to buyers (short calls now deep ITM, gamma collapses on those; new ATM options above create buying flow). Trend bias. Do not fade the move. Look for pullback-to-new-support (old Call Wall becomes new support) as your entry long. Target secondary Call Wall above. This is especially powerful when GEX is negative and VIX is falling.

Chapter Sixteen
How to Use the Walls in Your Trading
⚠️
Walls are context, not triggers

These levels define structural environment. They don't replace your volume profile triggers, VWAP setups, or absorption signals. They tell you where dealer flows will be strongest — improving probability of your existing playbooks.

📊
Scenario A — The Grinding Range Day

Setup: GEX positive. VIX 13 falling. NQ at 21050 — inside range (PW=20800, CW=21200). Histogram shows tall bars at both walls, thin in the middle.

→ PLAYBOOK: Fade approaches to Call Wall (21200) and Put Wall (20800). Tight targets near VWAP. Stop-run setups at extremes of the IB. This is the wall framework at its cleanest.
💥
Scenario B — The Breakdown Day

Setup: GEX negative. VIX 27 rising from 19 yesterday. NQ broke below Put Wall (20800) at open. Air pocket visible to 20600 (secondary Put Wall) on histogram.

→ PLAYBOOK: Trend-follow short. Target 20600 (secondary PW). Don't fade. Air pocket between 20800 and 20600 = fast move expected. Wider stops than normal.
😐
Scenario C — The Zero Gamma Hover

Setup: GEX near zero. VIX 17, flat. NQ oscillating at Zero Gamma level (21000) for 45 minutes. Thin histogram bar at 21000. Multiple wicks both directions.

→ PLAYBOOK: Wait. Do NOT trade the chop. Watch for volume expansion break of ZG with directional acceptance. Enter only after clear M15 close above or below with follow-through.
📅
Expiry Day Rules

Every Friday, and especially monthly third Fridays (triple expiry): walls can shift as contracts expire and new ones open. Max Pain gravity strongest in final hour (after 20:00 CET). Don't hold positions into final 90 minutes expecting walls to hold — their gamma is evaporating. New 5DTE walls reset Sunday night.

Chapter Seventeen
Translating QQQ Levels to Your NQ Chart
🔢
The relationship

QQQ is designed to trade at approximately 1/40th of the NDX (NASDAQ 100 cash index). NDX and NQ trade very close (usually within 10–40 points depending on futures premium). For GEX planning, treat NDX ≈ NQ.

🔢 THE CONVERSION FORMULA
NQ level ≈ QQQ level × 40
QQQ level ≈ NQ level ÷ 40
QQQ Call Wall at 480 → NQ ≈ 480 × 40 = 19,200
QQQ Put Wall at 460 → NQ ≈ 460 × 40 = 18,400
NQ at 21,100 → QQQ ≈ 21,100 ÷ 40 = 527.50

The TLADe indicator handles this conversion automatically on your NQ chart.
✅
Do you need to monitor QQQ directly?

No — not if you're using TLADe on your NQ chart. The indicator fetches QQQ options data and converts it to NQ levels automatically. All walls, Zero Gamma, Max Pain and Expected Move bands appear directly in NQ price terms. Use Barchart's QQQ page as a quick secondary sanity check only.

★ COMPLETE GLOSSARY ★
NQ
NASDAQ 100 Futures. 1 point = $20/contract. Tracks Apple, Microsoft, Nvidia and 97 others. 23hrs/day.
SPX
S&P 500 Index. 500 largest US companies. "The market." SPX options = world's largest options market = main GEX driver.
QQQ
Invesco QQQ ETF. Tracks NASDAQ 100. QQQ ≈ NDX ÷ 40 ≈ NQ ÷ 40. Your GEX data source.
NDX
NASDAQ 100 Cash Index. NDX ≈ NQ. QQQ = NDX ÷ 40. TLADe supports NDX directly.
VIX
Volatility Index. From SPX option prices (23-37 days). VIX ÷ 16 ≈ daily % move. Rising VIX activates vanna.
CBOE
Chicago Board Options Exchange. Calculates VIX. Largest options exchange globally.
GEX
Gamma Exposure. Net dealer gamma hedging. Positive = shock absorber. Negative = turbocharger. Chapter 9.
OPTION
Contract giving right (not obligation) to buy (call) or sell (put) at a specific price before a deadline. Buyer pays premium = max loss.
CALL OPTION
Right to BUY at strike. Profitable when market rises. Dealers short calls must sell NQ as price rises toward strike = Call Wall.
PUT OPTION
Right to SELL at strike. Insurance against falling prices. Dealers short puts must buy NQ as price falls = Put Wall.
STRIKE PRICE
Specific price an option targets. Options cluster at round numbers. High open interest at a strike = gamma wall.
PREMIUM
Fee paid to buy an option. Max loss for buyer. Higher VIX = higher premiums = more insurance value.
ITM / OTM
In/Out of The Money. ITM = intrinsic value now. OTM = only potential. Matters for vanna: ITM puts cause dealer NQ selling when VIX spikes.
THETA
Time decay. The daily erosion of option value as expiry approaches. Accelerates near expiry. 0DTE options have almost no theta — only pure price sensitivity remains.
IMPLIED VOLATILITY (IV)
The market's expectation of future movement embedded in option prices. High IV = expensive options. Rising IV = VIX spike = options get more expensive even without price moving.
VOL CRUSH
Sudden drop in IV after a known event resolves (earnings, Fed decision). Options collapse in price even if NQ moved "correctly." Vanna flows can reverse violently during vol crush.
DELTA
Three meanings: (1) option price change per NQ $1 move, (2) probability of expiring ITM, (3) dealer hedge ratio in NQ futures. ATM delta ≈ 0.50 = highest gamma = highest dealer risk.
GAMMA
Rate of change of delta per NQ point. Highest at ATM. High gamma = dealer must rebalance aggressively per tick = walls, pinning, magnetism.
VANNA
How delta changes when VIX changes. Active when VIX spikes. Causes dealer NQ selling into selloffs. "Gamma's evil twin." Explains crash acceleration and violent bear rallies.
DYNAMIC HEDGING
Continuous adjustment of dealer's NQ futures position as price moves. Not a one-time hedge — rebalanced constantly. Creates ongoing, predictable futures flow on your chart.
0DTE
Zero Days To Expiration. Extreme gamma. Peak during NY RTH (15:30+ CET). Sandcastle walls — reset daily. Not relevant for London session planning.
5DTE
Five Days To Expiration. Weekly options. Stable all week. Brick wall structure. Your primary GEX tool for London session. TLADe uses these by default.
OPEN INTEREST
Number of option contracts at a given strike. High OI = more dealer hedging = stronger wall = taller histogram bar.
CALL WALL
Strike with most call open interest. Resistance ceiling. Dealers sell NQ as price approaches. Break above = dealers flip to buying = move accelerates.
PUT WALL
Strike with most put open interest. Support floor. Dealers buy NQ as price approaches. Break below = gamma collapses = fast drop to secondary Put Wall.
ZERO GAMMA
Price where aggregate dealer gamma = zero. Regime switch. Above = shock absorber mode. Below = turbocharger mode. Most important intraday structural signal.
MAX PAIN
Strike where all options lose most total value at expiration. Options sellers profit most here. Price gravitates here on expiry day especially final hour.
GEX HISTOGRAM
Bar chart showing gamma concentration at every strike. Tall bar = strong wall. No bar = air pocket = fast moves with no dealer cushion.
AIR POCKET
Gap in GEX histogram. No dealer gamma cushion. Price moves fast in either direction. Best momentum entry zone after a wall break.
EXPECTED MOVE
Statistical range bands (±1 SD) derived from option pricing. Weekly outer boundaries of normal behaviour. TLADe shows these as EH/EL levels.
PINNING
Price oscillating around a heavy strike. Visible as wicks + no follow-through on M15. Don't trade the chop — wait for the directional break.
