The Jargon Dictionary — Read This First
📈
NQ

NASDAQ 100 Futures. The contract you trade. Tracks Apple, Microsoft, Nvidia, Amazon, Google, Meta and 94 others. Uses leverage, trades 23hrs/day. One NQ point = $20 profit/loss per contract.

📊
SPX

S&P 500 Index. 500 largest US companies combined. "The market" on the news. Moves with NQ. SPX options are the world's largest options market — they are the main driver of GEX flows.

🏦
QQQ

Invesco QQQ ETF. A share that tracks the NASDAQ 100. Big institutions buy/sell QQQ options to hedge their tech portfolios. Their hedging flows directly into NQ futures. QQQ is your main GEX data source.

📦
ETF

Exchange-Traded Fund. A basket of stocks in one share. QQQ = NASDAQ 100 stocks. SPY = S&P 500 stocks. Trades like a regular stock. ETF options markets are the link between options positioning and futures price action.

📜
NDX

NASDAQ 100 Cash Index. The "spot" price of the NASDAQ 100 without futures premium. NDX ≈ NQ (futures price is usually very close). QQQ = NDX ÷ 40. Important for the GEX conversion formula.

😰
VIX

Volatility Index — the "Fear Index." Measures expected SPX volatility over next 30 days. High VIX = fear = big moves expected. Low VIX = calm = small moves. Calculated by CBOE from live option prices. VIX ÷ 16 ≈ expected daily % move.

🏛️
CBOE

Chicago Board Options Exchange. World's largest options exchange. Where most SPX options trade. Publishes VIX in real time. All VIX numbers you see anywhere originate from CBOE's live calculation.

📐
GEX

Gamma Exposure. Net total of all dealer gamma hedging summed into one number. Positive = dealers dampen moves. Negative = dealers amplify moves. Your daily session character filter. Full explanation in Chapter 4.

⏰
0DTE

Zero Days To Expiration. Options expiring today. Extreme gamma — tiny NQ moves force massive dealer re-hedging. Volume has exploded since 2021. Main driver of intraday pinning. Most relevant during NY RTH (15:30 CET onwards).

📅
5DTE

Five Days To Expiration. Weekly options. The TLADe GEX indicator ships pre-loaded with 5DTE levels — this is your primary data source for London session planning. Refreshed weekly.

Most Important
What Actually IS an Option?
🏠 ANALOGY — HOUSE INSURANCE

You own a house worth €500,000. You're worried it might burn down, so you buy insurance for €2,000/year. The insurance gives you the right to sell your house for €500,000 — even if it burns down and is worth only €50,000.

You bought the right — not the obligation. If nothing happens, your insurance expires worthless and you lost €2,000. But if disaster strikes, that €2,000 policy is worth €450,000. A put option is exactly this — insurance on a stock or index.

📗
CALL Option

Right to BUY at a specific price. Bought when you think the market goes UP.

If NQ rises above the strike → call is valuable. If NQ stays below → call expires worthless, you lost the premium.

📕
PUT Option

Right to SELL at a specific price. Used as insurance when market might go DOWN.

If NQ falls below the strike → put is valuable. If NQ stays above → put expires worthless, you lost the premium.

💡
Key Terms

Strike Price: The specific price the option targets (e.g. 21000 NQ).
Expiry: The date the option runs out. After this, worthless if unused.
Premium: The fee you pay to buy the option. Your maximum possible loss.
ITM (In The Money): Option has real value right now.
OTM (Out of The Money): Option has only potential value, no intrinsic value yet.

01
WHY FUTURES TRADERS CARE
01
Chapter One
You're Already Swimming in Options Water
🎯
The Big Idea

Every day, thousands of options contracts are traded on SPX and QQQ — the indices your NQ tracks. The institutions who sold those options (dealers) must trade NQ futures to protect themselves. Their mechanical, predictable hedging trades affect your price action constantly.

"You don't need to trade options to be affected by them."
🤔
But I trade NQ futures — why do SPX/QQQ options matter?

Because SPX, QQQ and NQ track the same tech-heavy US market and move together almost tick-for-tick. When institutions hedge their options positions, they do it using NQ futures — the same market you're in. On active days, dealer hedging accounts for billions of dollars of NQ flow. That's not noise — it's a structural force shaping the entire session.

✅
What knowing this gives you

One quick pre-session check tells you whether today is likely to be a grinding, mean-reverting day (your fade setups work better) or an explosive trending day (your breakout setups work better). That single layer of context improves how you trade your existing playbooks.

02
THE CASINO — FULLY DECODED
02
Chapter Two
How Dealers Stay Neutral — Step by Step
🎰 THE BET

You walk into a casino and say: "I'll pay $50. If NQ goes up 100 points today, you pay me." The casino takes your bet. But the casino doesn't gamble — they immediately buy NQ futures to cover their risk. As NQ moves up or down during the day, they continuously adjust that futures position. They don't care which direction NQ goes. They just earn your $50 fee.

1️⃣
Step 1 — Your bet creates directional exposure

The casino now owes you money if NQ rises. In trading terms: they are effectively short NQ — they lose if NQ goes up. To offset this, they immediately buy NQ futures. Now if NQ goes up, their futures profit covers your payout. If NQ falls, they keep your $50 and their futures position is manageable.

2️⃣
Step 2 — A one-time hedge isn't enough

Here's what most people miss: your bet isn't linear like a simple futures contract. Your payout curve is curved (like an option). Below +100 points they owe you nothing. Above +100 points the payout starts and accelerates. This means their exposure (delta) changes as NQ moves. One static hedge doesn't keep them neutral for long.

3️⃣
Step 3 — Dynamic Hedging: the key concept

Because their delta changes continuously, they must adjust their futures position continuously:

📈 NQ moves up → their liability increases → they BUY more NQ futures
📉 NQ moves down → their liability shrinks → they SELL some NQ futures back

This is dynamic hedging. Every adjustment is a real futures trade in your market. The casino is not predicting NQ — it is manufacturing your payoff in the futures market.

💰
Step 4 — Where the casino actually makes money

They profit from edge, not direction:
• The fee (premium) you paid — priced so expected payout is less than the fee
• Bid-ask spread across many bettors
• Volatility edge — if the market moves less than they priced, they keep the difference

Conclusion: Dealers stay neutral by continuously rebalancing a futures hedge as price moves. Their rebalancing IS your price action.

⚡
HOW DEALERS AMPLIFY vs DAMPEN: When GEX is positive, dealers buy dips and sell rallies (shock absorbers). When GEX is negative, they buy rallies and sell dips (turbochargers). Same mechanical process — opposite market effect depending on their net position.
03
DELTA, GAMMA & VANNA
03
Chapter Three
3 Words — That's All You Need
DELTA
How much the dealer's NQ futures hedge must change when NQ moves $1. Changes continuously as price moves — forcing dealers to constantly rebalance.
🔵 WHY YOU CARE: Every NQ move forces dealer re-hedging. More delta = more predictable futures flow hitting your market.
GAMMA
How fast delta changes per NQ point. Highest when options are close to their strike price. High gamma = dealer must trade aggressively for each small price move.
🟠 WHY YOU CARE: High gamma at a strike = heavy dealer flow near that price = pinning, magnetic behaviour, walls.
VANNA
How much delta changes when VIX changes — not when price moves. When fear (VIX) spikes, deltas shift and dealers must trade NQ even without a big price move.
🟣 WHY YOU CARE: NQ falling + VIX rising = vanna forces more NQ selling. Explains why selloffs accelerate when fear spikes.
🏋️ ANALOGY — GAMMA

Imagine you're carrying a ball that gets heavier the closer you get to a wall. Near the wall (= near a strike price), every small step forces you to drop more weight to stay balanced. Far from the wall, big steps barely change anything.

That's gamma: the closer NQ is to a major strike, the more dealer re-hedging happens per point of price movement. This is why price slows near major strikes — and why the break, when it happens, is so sharp. The dealer's rebalancing has been building up like a coiled spring.

🌡️ ANALOGY — VANNA

Imagine your hedging needs change not because you moved, but because the room temperature changed. Even though you're standing perfectly still, you need to suddenly adjust your balance because the environment around you shifted.

That's vanna: VIX rising forces dealer re-hedging even when NQ price itself hasn't moved much yet. This is why a VIX spike triggers NQ selling before price has even had a chance to break a level — the environment changed, and dealers had no choice but to act.

05
VIX — HOW IT'S DERIVED
05
Chapter Five
VIX — The Market's Live Fear Thermometer
⚙️
How VIX is derived (plain English)

The CBOE looks at real-time prices of hundreds of SPX call and put options covering a wide range of strikes, with expirations 23–37 days out. For each option, they ask: "How much expected volatility does this option's price imply?"

They weight all of those together and express the result as an annualised percentage. VIX 20 = the market expects ±20% annual movement in SPX, which is roughly ±1.25% per day.

Quick formula: VIX ÷ 16 ≈ expected daily % move. VIX 16 → ~1%/day. VIX 32 → ~2%/day.

📍
What VIX is NOT

VIX is not directional — it doesn't say up or down. It measures expected violence of movement. It's also not a prediction of the future — it measures what current option prices imply right now. Direction matters too: a rising VIX activates vanna flows (Chapter 3). A falling VIX can trigger sharp bear market rallies.

VIX LEVELWHAT IT MEANSYOUR SESSION BIAS

⚡
POWER COMBO: VIX rising + GEX negative on same day = most explosive trend days of the year. VIX low/falling + GEX high positive = grinding range days where nothing works and fades rule.
06
PUT WALL, CALL WALL, ZERO GAMMA, MAX PAIN
06
Chapter Six
The Four Levels That Define the Day's Map
📗
CALL WALL

The strike with the most call open interest. Acts as resistance ceiling. Dealers who sold calls must sell NQ futures as price rises toward this level — creating mechanical selling. The stronger the open interest, the stronger the wall.

Price above Call Wall: Dealers flip to buying. Move can accelerate sharply upward. A new Call Wall resets at the next major strike above.

📕
PUT WALL

The strike with the most put open interest. Acts as support floor. Dealers who sold puts must buy NQ futures as price falls toward this level — creating mechanical buying. The stronger the open interest, the stronger the floor.

Price below Put Wall: Those puts go deep ITM, gamma collapses, mechanical buying fades. Moves below Put Wall are often fast and violent.

🔄
ZERO GAMMA (Gamma Flip)

The price level where aggregate dealer gamma crosses from positive to negative. The regime switch.

Price above Zero Gamma: Dealers stabilise (buy dips, sell rallies). Low volatility.
Price below Zero Gamma: Dealers amplify (sell dips, buy rallies). High volatility. This crossing is the most important intraday regime signal.

😖
MAX PAIN

The strike where the total value of all outstanding options is at its lowest — meaning options sellers (dealers) profit the most at this price.

Price gravitates toward Max Pain as expiration approaches because dealer hedging flows naturally pull it there. Strongest effect in the last hour of expiry day. Less relevant for your London session, but useful context on Fridays.

📐
The Expected Move Bands

The TLADe indicator also shows Expected Move (EM) High and Low. These are statistical bands derived from option pricing — roughly the 1-standard-deviation range the options market expects for the week. Think of them as the outer boundaries of normal price behaviour. Moves outside these bands are unusual and often violent.

Chapter Seven
Reading the GEX Profile — Not Just Where the Walls Are, But How Strong
📊
The GEX Histogram — what it is

The GEX indicator shows not just the location of walls, but the magnitude of gamma at every strike. This is displayed as a horizontal bar chart (histogram) directly on your NQ chart. The length of each bar tells you how strongly dealers must hedge at that exact level.

🔍
How to read it for your trading

Tall bar = strong wall. Dealer hedging will be aggressive here. Price will stall, struggle, and often reverse. The taller the bar, the more conviction you can have fading an approach to that level.

Short bar = weak level. Dealer hedging is thin here. Price passes through these levels easily — don't expect support/resistance from them.

Gap in the histogram = air pocket. A zone with no significant gamma. If price enters this zone, it moves quickly with no dealer cushion — either up or down. These gaps between levels are where the fastest NQ moves happen.

🎯
The Walls Beyond the Primary Range — Why They Matter

Most days, price stays between the Put Wall (floor) and Call Wall (ceiling). But when a wall breaks, the next wall becomes your map:

If Put Wall breaks: The next put wall below is your next support and your target. The distance between Put Wall and Secondary Put Wall tells you how far this move could run before finding new dealer buying. This is how you size targets on breakdown days.

If Call Wall breaks: The next call wall above becomes resistance and target. A break above the primary Call Wall with continuation to the secondary is a powerful multi-R move — dealers are forced buyers all the way up.

The histogram shows you all the walls, not just the primary ones. Reading the whole profile gives you targets at every level of escalation.

Walls are context, not triggers

These levels define the structural environment. They don't replace your volume profile triggers, VWAP setups, or absorption signals. They tell you where dealer flows will be strongest — which affects whether your playbooks are high or low probability.

🎯
The Range Play — Most Actionable Daily Setup

Each morning, note the Put Wall and Call Wall from the TLADe indicator on your NQ chart. The space between them is the statistical expected range for the day. Price at the bottom of this range → look for longs toward Call Wall. Price at the top → look for shorts toward Put Wall. Confluence with your VWAP levels and Volume Profile VAH/VAL turns these into high-probability setups.

📅
Expiry days are different

On options expiry days (especially every Friday with weekly 0DTE, and monthly third Fridays), walls can shift as contracts expire and new ones open. Pinning toward Max Pain is strongest in the final hour. Walls established in the morning can break violently in the afternoon as expiring contracts are closed. Be extra cautious holding positions into the final 90 minutes on expiry days.

QQQ → NQ CONVERSION
09
Chapter Nine
Translating QQQ Levels to Your NQ Chart
🔢
The relationship explained

QQQ is designed to trade at approximately 1/40th of the NDX (NASDAQ 100 cash index). NDX and NQ (futures) trade very close to each other — the spread is usually around 10–40 points depending on time to futures expiration. In practice, for GEX level planning, you can treat NDX ≈ NQ.

🔢 THE CONVERSION FORMULA
NQ level ≈ QQQ level × 40
QQQ level ≈ NQ level ÷ 40
Example: QQQ Call Wall at 480 → NQ ≈ 480 × 40 = 19,200
Example: QQQ Put Wall at 460 → NQ ≈ 460 × 40 = 18,400
Example: NQ at 21,100 → QQQ ≈ 21,100 ÷ 40 = 527.50

The TLADe indicator does this conversion automatically — you don't need to do the maths manually.
⚠️
Important nuance

The ×40 formula is an approximation. The exact NQ-to-NDX relationship shifts slightly as futures contracts roll (typically quarterly). The TLADe indicator accounts for this automatically by using a 40-point NQ-NDX spread in its conversion logic. For most trading purposes, ×40 is accurate enough. The TLADe indicator also lets you select whether you're on NQ, NDX, or QQQ directly — saving you any manual conversion.

✅
Do you need to monitor QQQ directly?

No — not if you're using the TLADe indicator on your NQ chart. The indicator fetches QQQ options data and converts it automatically to NQ levels. All walls, Zero Gamma, Max Pain, and Expected Move bands appear directly on your NQ chart in NQ price terms. You only look at Barchart's QQQ page as a secondary reference or sanity check.

How fast delta changes per NQ point. Highest near strike price. High gamma = heavy dealer flow per price tick = walls, pinning, magnetic behaviour.
VANNA
How much delta changes when VIX changes. Active when VIX spikes. Forces dealer NQ selling into selloffs. Why crashes accelerate. "Gamma's evil twin."
GEX
Gamma Exposure. Net of all dealer gamma hedging. Positive = dampen (stabiliser). Negative = amplify (turbocharger). Your primary session character filter.
CALL WALL
Strike with highest call open interest. Resistance ceiling. Dealers sell NQ as price approaches. Break above = dealers flip to buying = acceleration upward.
PUT WALL
Strike with highest put open interest. Support floor. Dealers buy NQ as price approaches. Break below = gamma collapses = fast move down to next put wall.
ZERO GAMMA
The price where aggregate dealer gamma crosses zero. Also called Gamma Flip or Volatility Trigger. Above = stabilising regime. Below = amplifying regime.
MAX PAIN
The strike where all outstanding options lose the most total value at expiration — where options sellers profit most. Price gravitates here on expiry day, especially the final hour.
GEX HISTOGRAM
A bar chart showing gamma concentration at every strike. Tall bar = strong wall. Short bar = weak level. Gap = air pocket with no dealer cushion = fast moves.
AIR POCKET
A gap in the GEX histogram with no significant gamma concentration. Price moves through these zones quickly in either direction with no dealer cushion to slow it down.
EXPECTED MOVE
Statistical range bands derived from option pricing. ~1 standard deviation range for the week. Price outside these bands is unusual and often volatile.
DELTA HEDGING
The continuous process of dealers adjusting their NQ futures position as price moves to stay delta-neutral. The mechanical engine behind all GEX effects.
DYNAMIC HEDGING
The fact that delta hedging must be done continuously — not just once. As NQ moves, delta changes and dealers must rebalance. Creates ongoing, predictable futures flow.
PINNING
Price gravitating toward a heavy strike and oscillating there. Caused by gamma concentration. Visible as wicks + no follow-through on your M15 chart. Don't trade the chop — wait for the break.
